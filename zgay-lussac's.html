Introduction to Gay-Lussac's Law

Gay-Lussac's Law, discovered by Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac in 1802, describes the relationship between the pressure and temperature of a gas when its volume is kept constant.

Gay-Lussac found that when the temperature of a gas increases, so does its pressure, and when the temperature decreases, the pressure drops as well. This happens because heating the gas makes the particles move faster, causing more frequent and forceful collisions with the walls of the container, thus increasing pressure.

Like Charles' Law, Gay-Lussac's work helped build the foundation for the ideal gas law. His law also played an important role in understanding the behavior of gases in various real-world situations, like in pressurized containers and steam engines.

   "When the temperature of a gas increases, so does its pressure, while the volume remains constant."

Temperature and pressure increase together in a gas because of the way gas particles behave. When a gas is heated, the particles gain energy and move faster. As they move more quickly, they collide with the walls of their container more often and with greater force.
These more frequent and stronger collisions lead to an increase in pressure. Since pressure is the result of these collisions, raising the temperature causes the gas to exert more pressure, provided the volume of the container stays the same.

Boyle's Law

Pressure & Volume

a connection between pressure and volume. It asserts that under constant temperature, the pressure of a specific quantity of a certain gas is inversely proportional to its volume.

Charles' Law

Volume & Temperature

A statement that the volume occupied by a fixed amount of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature, if the pressure remains constant, and temperature changes occur uniformly.

Combined Gas Laws

Pressure, Volume, & Temperature

A mix of Boyle's law, Charles' law and Gay-Lussac's law. The relationship of pressure, volume, and temperature: As pressure increases, temperature increases and volume decreases, following the ideal gas law principles.

Ideal Gas Law

Pressure & Volume

A principle which states that the pressure, volume, and temperature of an ideal gas are directly related. both pressure and volume are directly proportional to temperature individually under constant condition
