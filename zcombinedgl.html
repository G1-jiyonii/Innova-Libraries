Introduction to Boyle's Law

Boyle's Law describes the relationship between the pressure and volume of a gas. It states that the volume of a gas is inversely proportional to its pressure when the temperature remains constant. This means that as pressure increases, volume decreases, and vice versa.

The law was first documented by Robert Boyle in 1662 through experiments using a J-shaped tube filled with mercury. Edme Mariotte also independently discovered the same law in 1676. Isaac Newton provided an initial mathematical explanation, but the kinetic theory of gases later offered a more complete understanding of the relationship between pressure, volume, and temperature.

Boyle's Law

Pressure & Volume

a connection between pressure and volume. It asserts that under constant temperature, the pressure of a specific quantity of a certain gas is inversely proportional to its volume.

Charles' Law

Volume & Temperature

A statement that the volume occupied by a fixed amount of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature, if the pressure remains constant, and temperature changes occur uniformly.




Gay Lussac's Law

Pressure & Temperature

A statement that the pressure of a given mass of a gas varies directly with the absolute temperature of the gas, that is - if the volume is kept constant, and the gas behaves ideally.

Ideal Gas Law

Pressure & Volume

A principle which states that the pressure, volume, and temperature of an ideal gas are directly related. both pressure and volume are directly proportional to temperature individually under constant condition
