Introduction to Charles' Law

Charles's Law states that the volume of a given mass of gas varies directly with the absolute temperature of the gas when pressure is kept constant. The absolute temperature is temperature measured with the Kelvin scale. The Kelvin scale must be used because zero on the Kelvin scale corresponds to a complete stoppage of molecular motion.

Jacques Charles discovered his law in the late 18th century, around 1787. He was conducting experiments with gases, particularly hydrogen, and noticed something interesting: when a gas was heated, it expanded, and when it cooled, it contracted. This led him to the conclusion that the volume of a gas increases as its temperature rises, as long as the pressure stays the same.

While Charles was the first to make this observation, it wasn't immediately recognized or widely known. His findings were later confirmed and built upon by other scientists, making his work a key part of the foundation of gas laws in chemistry and physics.

   "When at constant pressure, the volume of a gas increases while temperature increases as well."

When the temperature of a gas increases, its particles gain more energy and move faster. This increased movement causes the particles to push away from each other, which makes the gas expand. As the gas expands, its volume increases. This happens because, at higher temperatures, the gas molecules collide with the container walls more frequently and with greater force, which causes the container to "stretch" or the gas to take up more space. So, when the temperature goes up, the gas needs more room to accommodate the faster-moving particles, leading to an increase in volume..

Boyle's Law

Pressure & Volume

a connection between pressure and volume. It asserts that under constant temperature, the pressure of a specific quantity of a certain gas is inversely proportional to its volume.

Gay Lussac's Law

Pressure & Temperature

A statement that the pressure of a given mass of a gas varies directly with the absolute temperature of the gas, that is - if the volume is kept constant, and the gas behaves ideally.

Combined Gas Laws

Pressure, Volume, & Temperature

A mix of Boyle's law, Charles' law and Gay-Lussac's law. The relationship of pressure, volume, and temperature: As pressure increases, temperature increases and volume decreases, following the ideal gas law principles.

Ideal Gas Law

Pressure & Volume

A principle which states that the pressure, volume, and temperature of an ideal gas are directly related. both pressure and volume are directly proportional to temperature individually under constant condition
