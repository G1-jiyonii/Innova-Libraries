Introduction to Ideal Gas Law

The Ideal Gas Law is an equation that describes the behavior of an ideal gas, combining several earlier gas laws into one. It was formulated in the 19th century, building on the work of scientists like Boyle, Charles, Gay-Lussac, and Avogadro. The law states that the pressure, volume, and temperature of a gas are all related in a simple way when the gas behaves ideally

The Ideal Gas Law evolved from the work of several key scientists who studied the behavior of gases. Robert Boyle's work in the 17th century demonstrated the inverse relationship between pressure and volume at constant temperature. Jacques Charles, in the 18th century, discovered that gas volume increases with temperature when pressure is constant, while Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac showed that gas pressure increases with temperature at constant volume.

Amedeo Avogadro's hypothesis in 1811 added that equal volumes of gas, at the same temperature and pressure, contain the same number of molecules, laying the foundation for the concept of the mole. In 1834, Émile Clapeyron combined these findings into the Ideal Gas Law, which provides a unified equation to describe the behavior of gases under various conditions, making it a cornerstone of thermodynamics and gas behavior studies.

The volume of a gas decreases when pressure increases because of the way gas molecules behave. When pressure is applied to a gas, the gas molecules are forced into a smaller space, causing them to collide more frequently with the walls of the container. Since gas molecules are in constant motion, increasing the pressure reduces the amount of space available for them to move, which results in a decrease in volume. This is a direct consequence of the gas molecules trying to maintain their constant motion while being compressed.

UP NEXT:

Boyle's Law

Pressure & Volume

a connection between pressure and volume. It asserts that under constant temperature, the pressure of a specific quantity of a certain gas is inversely proportional to its volume.

Charles' Law

Volume & Temperature

A statement that the volume occupied by a fixed amount of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature, if the pressure remains constant, and temperature occurs uniformly.

Gay Lussac's Law

Pressure & Temperature

A statement that the pressure of a given mass of a gas varies directly with the absolute temperature of the gas, that is - if the volume is kept constant, and the gas behaves ideally.

Combined Gas Laws

Pressure, Volume, & Temperature

A mix of Boyle's law, Charles' law and Gay-Lussac's law. The relationship of pressure, volume, and temperature: As pressure increases, temperature increases and volume decreases.
